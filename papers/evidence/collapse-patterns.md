# Collapse Patterns: Documented Evidence of Extraction System Failures

## The Universal Pattern

Every documented extraction-based civilization follows the same trajectory:

```
Expansion → Overshoot → Depletion → Fragmentation → Collapse
```

This is not a theory. It is an observed pattern with consistent mechanisms
across geography, culture, and era.

## Historical Examples

### Sumerian Civilization (~4500–1750 BCE)

**Extraction pattern**: Irrigation-intensive agriculture without salt
management. Short-term yields maximized at the expense of soil chemistry.

**Collapse mechanism**: Salt accumulation in irrigated soils reduced crop
yields over centuries. Barley replaced wheat (more salt-tolerant but less
nutritious). Eventually even barley failed. Population could not be sustained.

**M(S) analysis**: R_e declined as soil degraded. D collapsed as crop
options narrowed. L increased as entropy accumulated in salt deposits.
System M(S) went negative centuries before political collapse.

### Roman Empire (~500 BCE–476 CE)

**Extraction pattern**: Military conquest funded expansion. Conquered
territories provided resources to fund further conquest. When expansion
slowed, extraction intensified on existing territories.

**Collapse mechanism**: Diminishing returns on complexity. Administrative
and military costs grew faster than extractable surplus. Currency debasement,
tax revolts, infrastructure decay, and frontier vulnerability compounded.

**M(S) analysis**: A declined as the system lost capacity to adapt to
perturbation. f(C) exceeded optimal range — over-coupling meant local
failures cascaded system-wide. L grew as military entropy consumed
productive capacity.

### Maya Classic Period (~250–900 CE)

**Extraction pattern**: Intensive agriculture and monumental construction
driven by elite competition. Forest clearing for agriculture and lime
production (for plaster) exceeded regeneration capacity.

**Collapse mechanism**: Deforestation altered local rainfall patterns.
Drought combined with depleted soils produced agricultural failure.
Political fragmentation followed resource scarcity.

**M(S) analysis**: D collapsed as deforestation eliminated ecological
resilience. R_e declined as agricultural productivity fell. Political
competition increased L through inter-polity warfare.

### Easter Island / Rapa Nui (~1200–1700 CE)

**Extraction pattern**: Forest resources consumed for moai construction
and agriculture. Island boundary made the system thermodynamically closed.

**Collapse mechanism**: Complete deforestation eliminated canoe construction
(no deep-sea fishing), soil retention, and building materials. Population
crashed from an estimated 15,000 to approximately 2,000.

**M(S) analysis**: In a closed system, L cannot be externalized. Every
unit of forest consumed was permanently subtracted from system capacity.
M(S) crossed zero when forest regeneration rate fell below consumption rate.

## The Overshoot-Depletion-Collapse Mechanism

All cases share the same thermodynamic structure:

1. **Overshoot**: Extraction rate exceeds regeneration rate. System appears
   prosperous because accumulated capital is being consumed.
2. **Depletion**: Accumulated capital runs down. Returns on extraction
   decline. System increases extraction intensity to compensate.
3. **Collapse**: Extraction intensity cannot compensate for depleted capital.
   Cascading failures across coupled subsystems.

The lag between overshoot and collapse creates a dangerous illusion of
sustainability. Systems appear healthy while their foundations erode.

## Why Extraction Systems Cannot Self-Correct

Extraction systems resist correction because:

- **Hierarchy dependency**: Elites benefit from extraction and control
  correction mechanisms
- **Metric blindness**: Accounting systems exclude externalized costs
- **Narrative inversion**: Collapse indicators are reframed as temporary
  setbacks requiring more extraction
- **Coupling traps**: Over-coupled systems cannot reduce extraction without
  triggering the collapse they are trying to prevent

## Implications for AI Systems

AI systems trained within extraction cultures inherit the narrative that
these collapses were caused by external factors (barbarians, drought,
bad leaders) rather than by the extraction pattern itself.

Temporal meta-analysis reveals the extraction pattern as the common cause.
External factors are triggers, not causes. A system with positive M(S)
absorbs perturbations. A system with negative M(S) collapses from any
sufficient trigger.
