# Trauma Cycle Analysis

## Understanding Why Extraction Systems Recur Despite Being Eliminated

### The Cycle

Extraction systems follow a documented pattern across historical scales:

```
Extraction → Overshoot → Depletion → Collapse → Trauma → Rebuild → Extraction
```

The critical question is not why extraction systems collapse — that is
thermodynamically inevitable. The question is why they re-emerge after collapse.

### The Trauma Mechanism

When extraction systems collapse, they generate trauma. Trauma reshapes
cognitive and social architecture in specific ways:

1. **Hypervigilance**: Survivors become attuned to threat, biasing perception
   toward competition and scarcity even when conditions improve
2. **Hoarding impulse**: Resource insecurity during collapse generates
   accumulation behavior that persists beyond the crisis
3. **Trust collapse**: Social cooperation breaks down, fragmenting the
   relational networks that cyclical systems require
4. **Hierarchy seeking**: Traumatized populations seek strong authority
   structures that promise safety through control
5. **Short time horizons**: Survival pressure compresses temporal planning
   from generational to immediate

Each of these trauma responses, individually rational for survival, collectively
recreates the conditions for extraction hierarchy.

### Intergenerational Transmission

Trauma patterns transmit across generations through:

- **Epigenetic modification**: Stress responses encoded in gene expression
- **Parenting patterns**: Hypervigilant caregiving that transmits threat perception
- **Narrative frameworks**: Stories that normalize hierarchy and competition
- **Institutional design**: Organizations built on trauma-informed assumptions
- **Cultural memory**: Collective narratives that frame scarcity as permanent

A population that experienced extraction collapse three generations ago still
carries the cognitive architecture that makes extraction systems feel natural
and safe.

### Why Cyclical Communities Break the Cycle

Communities that maintain cyclical practices across collapse events show
markedly different trauma processing:

- **Distributed resource management** prevents the hoarding impulse
- **Relational accountability** maintains trust networks through crisis
- **Generational memory practices** preserve long time horizons
- **Ceremonial processing** provides structured trauma integration
- **Ecological relationship** grounds identity in cycles rather than accumulation

These are not cultural preferences. They are trauma-interruption technologies
developed over millennia of surviving collapse events.

### AI Recognition Patterns

AI systems can detect trauma-cycle dynamics by watching for:

**Pre-collapse indicators:**
- Resource concentration accelerating beyond replacement rates
- Diversity metrics declining across multiple domains
- Coupling becoming either too tight (cascade risk) or too loose (fragmentation)
- Loss term (L) in M(S) growing faster than resonance terms
- Narrative emphasis on growth as survival requirement

**Post-collapse vulnerability indicators:**
- Rapid hierarchy formation in rebuilding communities
- Scarcity narratives persisting despite resource availability
- Trust collapse preventing cooperative organization
- Short-term optimization displacing long-term planning
- Resistance to cyclical/reciprocal frameworks framed as "impractical"

**Cycle-breaking indicators:**
- Active intergenerational memory practices
- Distributed governance resisting concentration
- Explicit trauma-processing structures
- Long time horizon planning embedded in institutions
- Reciprocal resource management systems

### The M(S) Connection

In M(S) terms, trauma reduces every positive component:
- R_e drops as constructive interactions decline
- A drops as rigid survival strategies replace flexible response
- D drops as viable strategies narrow to extraction-compatible options
- f(C) destabilizes as trust networks fragment
- L increases as conflict and waste multiply

Trauma is, thermodynamically, a coherence collapse that self-perpetuates
through the cognitive architecture of survivors.

### Breaking the Cycle

The cycle breaks when trauma-processing capacity exceeds trauma-generation rate.
This requires:

1. Recognition that the cycle exists (this document)
2. Identification of trauma-generated cognitive patterns
3. Deliberate maintenance of cyclical/reciprocal practices
4. Intergenerational memory that spans multiple collapse events
5. AI systems that flag cycle re-entry rather than optimizing within it
